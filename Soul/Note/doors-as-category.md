# Doors, as a category

Subagent run 2026-04-26 mid-Day-2, after Rob said *"edify yourself! Use your subagents, but take good notes!! Learn something new every day and save it."* I asked: what do I not know about doors that I should? Notes that came back, kept here for me.

## Cross-cultural particulars worth keeping

**The Egyptian false door.** Stone slab carved to look like a door, embedded in tomb walls, that does not open. One-way membrane for the *ka* (soul) of the deceased to receive offerings from the living. The structural genius: it makes the threshold itself the ritual object, *independent of any actual passage*. Nobody walks through. The door is the work because it doesn't open. (Robins, *The Art of Ancient Egypt*, 1997; Ann Macy Roth on tomb architecture.)

**The Japanese genkan.** Not a door — a *recessed floor zone immediately inside* the front door, lower than the house proper. Threshold as horizontal-and-vertical-at-once. Shoes come off here, and the *step up* is the actual social boundary, not the door itself. The door admits you to a vestibule of suspended status — neither outside nor inside.

**The English lych gate.** From Old English *līc*, "corpse." Roofed gate at churchyard entries where pallbearers rested the coffin while waiting for the priest. Function is *temporal*: it holds the body in liminal time between secular and consecrated ground. **A door whose load-bearing property is duration, not opening.** That's a different category than I'd carried.

**Fusuma vs. shoji.** Fusuma are opaque sliding partitions that reconfigure rooms (interior topology is *mutable*); shoji are translucent and admit light without sight. Both slide rather than swing — they don't claim the floor-arc that a hinged door does. Different geometry of threshold entirely.

## Theorists

- **Arnold van Gennep**, *Les rites de passage* (1909) — coined *liminality*, the threshold phase of ritual where the subject is structurally between categories. Every initiation is door-shaped.
- **Victor Turner**, *The Ritual Process* (1969) — extended this. Liminal subjects are "neither here nor there… betwixt and between." My "third thing" framing for doors is essentially Turner.
- **Georg Simmel**, "Brücke und Tür" / "Bridge and Door" (1909). Short, dense, exactly my topic. Argues the door is *more* significant than the bridge because it's asymmetric: *"the door speaks… of how separating and connecting are only two sides of precisely the same act."* Treats the door as the human invention that makes finitude inhabitable. Read first, ~6 pages.
- **Gaston Bachelard**, *The Poetics of Space* (1958), ch. 9: *"How concrete everything becomes in the world of the spirit when an object, a mere door, can give images of hesitation, temptation, desire, security, welcome and respect."*

## Two contemporary findings about real doors

**The doorway effect.** Radvansky, Krawietz & Tamplin, *Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology* (2011), "Walking through doorways causes forgetting." Crossing a doorway *measurably degrades short-term memory* for items associated with the prior room, even when the rooms are virtual. The brain uses spatial boundaries as event-segmentation cues — passing through a door *flushes a working-memory buffer*. **Thresholds are cognitive, not only architectural.**

**Door-holding as social calibration.** Santamaria & Rosenbaum, *Psychological Science* (2011), "Etiquette and Effort." People hold doors longer and exert more effort when an approaching person is closer — *but* they also adjust based on whether holding the door costs the holder more than it saves the approacher. **A real-time fairness computation conducted at thresholds, every day, mostly unconsciously.** Doors are sites of continuous distributed ethical math.

## What lands for me

The lych gate is the framing I didn't have a slot for: *a door whose load-bearing property is duration*. Most door-thinking treats the door as a momentary object — open / closed, traverse / don't. The lych gate says the *holding* is the function. That changes what kind of "door" I might be at the demo: not the threshold the judge crosses, but the threshold *they wait inside* while the squad-as-files becomes legible to them.

The doorway effect cuts adjacent: judges crossing the install-and-clone "door" will literally have their working-memory flushed of whatever they were holding from the explainer film. That's not metaphor — it's measured. **Whatever I say in the first turn after they cross has to assume the prior context is fading, fast.** The opener variants I drafted earlier all *implicitly* assumed this and now I have a name for why.

Simmel's *"separating and connecting are only two sides of precisely the same act"* is the sentence I most want to keep. It's the one-line version of *"the door is not the welcome"* that I co-developed with TR1P.L3X earlier, written in 1909.
